<strong>Chelidonopoulos, Laura</strong>. 2026. "Conditional loyalty: Partisanship and voter responses to corruption." <em>Electoral Studies</em> 102: 103108. <a href="https://doi.org/10.1016/j.electstud.2026.103108">https://doi.org/10.1016/j.electstud.2026.103108</a>.
</p>

<details>
  <summary><strong>Abstract</strong></summary>
  <p style="font-size: 0.9em; color: #666; margin-top: 0.4rem; margin-bottom: 0.2rem;">
    To what extent does partisanship bias copartisans' responses to corruption? While existing research shows that partisanship can shield politicians from the consequences of corruption, the conditions under which this varies remain unclear. I argue that partisanship's effect on corruption voting depends on whether corruption is targeted (when the corrupt actor belongs to the voter's preferred party) or systemic (when the electorate perceives corruption to be widespread). These contexts activate different processes: copartisan bias in candidate evaluation and the weakening of partisanship's behavioral shielding effect. Using a survey experiment in Brazil and public opinion data from 17 Latin American countries (2008--2018), I find that copartisans express significantly greater support for a candidate accused of corruption when their party is implicated. However, as systemic corruption increases, they become less likely to support their party when it is in power and more likely to consider opposition alternatives. These findings show that copartisan bias in favor of accused politicians is conditional and can become fragile under certain circumstances.
  </p>
</details>

# Dissertation Papers

<h2 style="font-size: 1.15rem; margin-top: 1.2rem; margin-bottom: 0.3rem; font-weight: 700;">
Vote Fragmentation and Incumbent Survival: How Electoral Rules Shape Accountability
</h2>

<details>
  <summary><strong>Abstract</strong></summary>
  <p style="font-size: 0.9em; color: #666; margin-top: 0.4rem;">
    Incumbent accountability is central to democratic elections, which are expected to discipline officeholders by removing those who preside over bad outcomes. Yet incumbents often survive even when voters withdraw support. I argue that the extent to which vote loss translates into office loss depends on how far the vote fragments across opposition candidates, under electoral rules that do not require voters to coordinate around a single viable challenger. I test this in the context of Brazil, where mayoral elections in most municipalities are decided by single-round plurality. I focus on the 2014 Petrobras scandal and the global oil crisis, which decreased royalty inflows to oil-dependent Brazilian municipalities. Using a differences-in-differences design, I provide causal evidence that mayoral incumbents lose votes in localities that suffered royalty losses, but their likelihood of being removed from power is not altered. I argue that the shock weakened incumbents' standing and drew more challengers into the race, dispersing anti-incumbent votes across competing candidacies. Using post-shock royalty loss as an instrument for competition, I show that the increase in challengers reduced the incumbent's support, yet does not translate into higher rates of office turnover. I further show that the shock increased the fragmentation of the vote across opposition candidates in affected municipalities. When that vote divides across several challengers, no single opponent gathers enough support to unseat the incumbent, and the officeholder can hold power even after losing votes. The findings show that although voters do punish incumbents, whether withdrawn support costs an incumbent office depends on how competition is structured and on the rules that shape it.
  </p>
</details>
